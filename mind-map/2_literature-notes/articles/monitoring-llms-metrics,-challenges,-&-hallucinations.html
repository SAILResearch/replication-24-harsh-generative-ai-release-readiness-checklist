Monitoring LLMs: Metrics, Challenges, & Hallucinations

  • LLMs can be quite a handful. They’re big, complex, and the moment you think you’ve got them figured out, they throw a curveball. You want them to write like Hemingway but sometimes they seem to channel a philosophy major writing their first essay. (View Highlight)
  • Scale: The sheer size of LLMs means they generate heaps of data. Monitoring that tsunami of information is no easy task. (View Highlight)
  • Quality & accuracy: What’s a quality output? Shakespeare and modern-day texting are both English but hardly the same thing. Defining what’s “good” isn’t black and white. This goes the same for measuring your LLMs accuracy. (View Highlight)
  • Bias: These models can sometimes spew out biased or offensive content, and you want to catch that before it wreaks havoc. (View Highlight)
  • Hallucinations in LLMs are when they start making things up. Not cool, right? Imagine your model pulling “facts” out of thin air! You need to keep a sharp eye on this. Set up an anomaly detection system that flags unusual patterns in the responses. You can also have a moderation layer that cross-checks facts with a reliable source. (View Highlight)
  • Training Bias – Biases in training data and lack of diversity impede generalization, while their complex architecture challenges researchers in pinpointing and correcting these issues. (View Highlight)
  • Overfitting – Overfitting occurs when a LLM excessively adapts to its training data, resulting in a failure to generalize and potentially generating spurious, or “hallucinated,” content when faced with new, unseen data. (View Highlight)
  • Bad Prompts – When input prompts are murky, at odds, or self-contradictory, the stage is set for hallucinations to emerge. Although users don’t have a say in data quality and training, they hold the reins when it comes to input context. By sharpening their input game, users can nudge the results towards improvement. (View Highlight)
    • Note: Important for in-context learning
  • If users are reacting negatively or appear confused, take that as a sign. (View Highlight)
  • Prompts are like the breadcrumbs you give LLMs to follow. Sometimes, they take those crumbs and go off into a maze. To monitor this, keep an eye on how well the model’s responses align with the intent of the prompts. (View Highlight)
  • human-in-the-loop to validate a subset of responses or set up a system that scores alignment and flags any that are drifting off into the weeds. (View Highlight)
    • Note: possible to use LLM for evaluation
  • Key metrics to track for Large Language Models (View Highlight)
  • Perplexity: This metric is akin to your LLM’s stress ball. It tells you how confused the model is with the tasks it’s trying to accomplish. Lower perplexity typically means the model is more confident in its outputs. (Not relevant to anyone using Open AI’s ChatGPT) (View Highlight)
  • Token Efficiency: LLMs, particularly models like GPT, consume tokens for both input and output. Tokens can be as short as one character or as long as one word. Monitoring how efficiently an LLM uses tokens is essential for controlling costs and ensuring that the model doesn’t exceed its maximum token limit. (View Highlight)
  • For example, if a model has a maximum token limit of N, and it uses M tokens for a given input-output pair, the token efficiency could be represented as the ratio: Token Efficiency = M/N (View Highlight)
  • Response Time: Be considerate of your users’ time. You don’t want your LLM to be that slowpoke who takes ages to respond. (View Highlight)
  • Accuracy and F1 Score: You need to know how often your model is hitting the bullseye or missing the mark. Accuracy gives you an idea of how often your model is right, while the F1 Score provides a balance between precision (how many of the predicted positives are actually positive) and recall (how many actual positives were predicted correctly). (View Highlight)
  • (only relevant for certain AI tasks, for example using LLMs for classification.) (View Highlight)
  • Throughput: When measuring the efficiency of your LLM, it is important to consider throughput – the number of requests or operations your model can handle per unit of time. This is particularly crucial in high-traffic environments where a high volume of requests needs to be processed swiftly. (View Highlight)
  • Drift: Changes in the data distribution over time can lead to a decrease in model performance, a phenomenon known as “drift”. Be sure to keep an eye on your model’s inputs and outputs for any changes. This can be crucial in the early detection of shifts in user behavior or environmental changes that your model needs to adapt to. (View Highlight)
  • There isn’t a single formula for drift, but it typically involves comparing the performance of the model on current data to its performance on historical data. (View Highlight)
  • Fairness Metrics: LLMs are known to sometimes carry and perpetuate biases found in the data they were trained on. Organizations should monitor and quantify biases in model outputs using fairness metrics. This can be domain-specific and include metrics like gender bias, racial bias, or other forms of unintended bias. (View Highlight)
  • However, they generally involve comparing the performance or outcomes of the model across different demographic groups. For example, Gender Bias can be measured by comparing the model’s performance for male vs. female inputs. (View Highlight)
