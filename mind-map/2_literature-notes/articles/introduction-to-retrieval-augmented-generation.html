Introduction to Retrieval Augmented Generation

  • LLMs are trained on internet-scale data across a large set of sources that are publicly available. These data sets are similar to the open crawl data sources, such as the common crawl. (View Highlight)
  • An LLM won’t know the specifics of your product documentation or understand the unique workings of your product. If there are updates or changes to your product, an LLM won’t automatically have that information unless it’s included in its training data. (View Highlight)
  • Let’s bust that myth: in practice, most data augmentation is accomplished through prompt engineering or search and retrieval methods using a vector database. (View Highlight)
  • The results are staggering, with significantly higher accuracy than applications without search and retrieval and less load than fine-tuning the model. (View Highlight)
  • Finally, by focusing on semantically relevant chunks, the LLM can provide more precise and contextually appropriate responses, leading to improved customer satisfaction. (View Highlight)
  • Production: Common Problems With Search and Retrieval Systems (View Highlight)
  • One such challenge arises when a user inputs a query that doesn’t closely match any chunks in the vector store. The system looks for a needle in a haystack but finds no needle at all. This lack of match, often caused by unique or highly specific queries, can leave the system to draw on the “most similar” chunks available – ones that aren’t entirely relevant. (View Highlight)
  • Monitoring three main things can help prevent these issues: (View Highlight)
  • Query Density (Drift): Query density refers to how well user queries are covered by the vector store. If query density drifts significantly, it signals that our vector store may not be capturing the full breadth of user queries, resulting in a shortage of closely associated chunks. (View Highlight)
  • Ranking Metrics: These metrics evaluate how well the search and retrieval system is performing in terms of selecting the most relevant chunks. (View Highlight)
  • User Feedback: Encouraging users to provide feedback on the quality and relevance of the LLM’s responses helps gauge user satisfaction and identify areas for improvement. (View Highlight)
  • Refinement: How to Optimize and Improve Search and Retrieval (View Highlight)
  • Key areas to focus on include: (View Highlight)
  • Chunking Strategy: Evaluating how information is broken down and processed during this stage can help highlight areas for improvement in performance. (View Highlight)
  • Retrieval Performance: Assessing how well the system retrieves information can indicate if you need to employ different tools or strategies, such as context ranking or HYDE. (View Highlight)
  • Upon release, optimization efforts should continue as you enter the post-production phase. Even after launch, with a well-defined evaluation strategy, you can proactively identify any emerging issues and continue to improve your model’s performance. Consider approaches like: (View Highlight)
  • Expanding your Knowledge Base: Adding documentation can significantly improve your system’s response quality. An expanded data set allows your LLM to provide more accurate and tailored responses. (View Highlight)
  • Refining Chunking Strategy: Further modifying the way information is broken down and processed can lead to marked improvements. (View Highlight)
  • Enhancing Context Understanding: Incorporating an extra ‘context evaluation’ step helps the system incorporate the most relevant context into the LLM’s response. (View Highlight)
